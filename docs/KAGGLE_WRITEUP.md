# Sentinel: Climate-Aware Autonomous Investing with Multi-Agent Systems
**(Kaggle Freestyle Track Submission)**

## 1. The Problem: The Climate Risk Blindspot in Modern Investing
In the rapidly evolving landscape of modern finance, automated investing platforms—often dubbed "robo-advisors"—have democratized wealth management. However, these systems operate under a glaring blindspot: they optimize almost entirely for historical returns and volatility, treating climate risk as a peripheral, non-quantifiable externality. The reality is that climate change is no longer just an environmental issue; it is a systemic financial risk. Regulatory shifts, carbon pricing, and physical climate impacts are fundamentally altering asset valuations. 

Conversely, dedicated ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) funds attempt to address this but often fall short. They rely on rigid, opaque scoring methodologies that update infrequently and fail to account for real-time market dynamics. Investors are forced into a frustrating compromise. If they choose traditional automated investing, they unwittingly expose their portfolios to significant, unpriced climate risks. If they choose dedicated ESG funds, they sacrifice the agility and responsiveness necessary to navigate volatile markets effectively.

The core of the problem lies in the inability of current systems to synthesize two fundamentally different data streams: high-frequency financial market signals and complex, qualitative climate impact data. Traditional algorithmic trading models cannot interpret the nuance of a company's net-zero transition plan, nor can static ESG spreadsheets react to a sudden earnings report or geopolitical market shock. What is required is a system capable of simultaneous, nuanced reasoning across both domains—a system that can ingest real-time market data, weigh it against strict environmental constraints, simulate the resulting portfolio risk, and execute trades, all without human intervention. This is the problem Sentinel was built to solve. By treating climate impact as a primary vector for financial risk rather than a moral afterthought, Sentinel aims to prove that sustainable investing does not have to come at the cost of market agility.

## 2. Why Agents: The Limitation of the Monolithic LLM
When I initially set out to solve the dual-domain problem of climate-aware investing, the most obvious approach was to use a single, powerful Large Language Model. The hypothesis was simple: feed the LLM a prompt containing both financial data and ESG scores, and ask it to output a trading decision. It quickly became apparent that this monolithic approach was fundamentally flawed.

A single LLM, when asked to juggle complex mathematical portfolio optimization, qualitative ESG analysis, and strict compliance policy enforcement, inevitably suffers from context collapse and hallucination. When the prompt grew to include real-time stock prices, historical volatility metrics, carbon intensity limits, and user budget constraints, the model would frequently drop critical instructions. It might authorize a trade for a fossil fuel company because the stock chart looked bullish, completely ignoring the strict environmental exclusion policy buried in paragraph three of the system prompt. Or, conversely, it would become overly focused on the ESG score and recommend an allocation that mathematically exceeded the user's total budget.

This is why Sentinel requires a multi-agent architecture. By decomposing the massive cognitive load into a sequence of specialized agents, we establish a robust division of labor. An agent instructed solely to be a "Climate Expert" does not need to worry about Alpaca API trade execution; it focuses entirely on extracting and analyzing carbon footprints. A "Portfolio Agent" receives the synthesized climate and financial data and focuses purely on mathematical allocation. Most importantly, a multi-agent system allows for adversarial checks and balances. In a single LLM, if the model decides to buy ExxonMobil, the trade executes. In Sentinel, the Trader Agent might propose the ExxonMobil trade, but it must pass through the independent Guard Agent, whose sole directive is to veto non-compliant proposals. This separation of concerns is the only way to build a reliable, autonomous financial system.

## 3. Architecture: The Sentinel ADK Pipeline
Sentinel is built on the Agent Development Kit (ADK), utilizing a `SequentialAgent` graph to orchestrate a linear pipeline of seven highly specialized sub-agents. This architecture ensures that every user request—whether a simple query or a complex trade authorization—passes through a rigorous gauntlet of analysis, simulation, and security checks before any action is taken.

The pipeline is initiated when the user submits a request. The first node is the **Climate Agent**. Armed with the Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite model, this agent's sole responsibility is environmental evaluation. It connects to the ESG MCP Server to fetch carbon footprints, evaluate net-zero targets, and classify the asset against green sector taxonomies. It outputs a pure climate profile. 

This profile is passed to the **Financial Agent**, which focuses on market realities. Connecting to the Market MCP Server, it retrieves live stock prices and assesses market trends based on the user's risk tolerance. It ignores the climate data, acting purely as a traditional financial analyst to determine the asset's current market viability.

With both profiles generated, the context flows to the **Simulation Agent**. This agent acts as the system's stress-tester. It takes the proposed assets and runs a Monte Carlo simulation based on the user's budget and projected volatility. This ensures that the potential investment is mathematically sound over a simulated time horizon.

The synthesized data—climate, financial, and simulation—is then handed to the **Portfolio Agent**. This agent acts as the allocator. It takes the user's total budget and determines the optimal distribution across the eligible, climate-approved assets. It outputs a structured allocation strategy.

Next, the **Trader Agent** translates this allocation strategy into actionable paper trade proposals. It formats the data specifically for the Alpaca trading API, calculating exact share quantities based on the live prices fetched earlier. Crucially, the Trader Agent does not execute the trade; it only proposes it.

This brings us to the most critical component: the **Guard Agent**. Positioned as an immutable checkpoint just before execution, the Guard Agent enforces the system's security and compliance layer. It interfaces with the Policy MCP Server to retrieve active trading constraints (e.g., maximum portfolio concentration, excluded sectors like weapons or fossil fuels). The Guard Agent evaluates the Trader's proposal against these policies. If a violation is detected, the Guard Agent blocks the trade, writes an event to the immutable audit log, and halts the pipeline. If the proposal passes, the trade is authorized for paper execution.

Finally, the **Explain Agent** receives the complex JSON state of the entire pipeline. Its job is to translate the internal machinations—why an asset was chosen, what the Monte Carlo simulation revealed, and whether the Guard Agent blocked the trade—into a clear, human-readable report for the user.

Supporting these agents are three isolated **Model Context Protocol (MCP) Servers**:
- The **ESG Server** provides the environmental data layer.
- The **Market Server** handles live financial ticker data.
- The **Policy Server** manages the system's trading constraints and audit logging.
These servers run as child subprocesses over STDIO, completely decoupling the tool execution logic from the agent reasoning loop.

## 4. Implementation Journey: From OpenClaw to ADK
The development of Sentinel was not a straight path; it was a journey of necessary iteration. My initial prototype was built using a custom, lightweight framework I called "OpenClaw." OpenClaw was essentially a wrapper around standard API calls, using basic prompt chaining to pass data between a couple of LLM calls. While it proved the core concept—that an LLM could analyze both financial and ESG data—it collapsed under its own weight as the system grew. Managing the state between calls became a nightmare of brittle dictionary parsing. Adding new tools required hardcoding complex JSON schemas directly into the prompt logic. It lacked robust telemetry, state management, and most importantly, a standardized way for agents to interface with external tools.

Migrating to the Agent Development Kit (ADK) was a paradigm shift. The ADK provided the robust scaffolding required to build a true multi-agent system. The most immediate benefit was the `SequentialAgent` graph. Instead of manually parsing outputs and piping them to the next function, the ADK allowed me to define a clear, linear DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph) where the context automatically cascaded from the Climate Agent down to the Explain Agent.

Simultaneously, I adopted the Model Context Protocol (MCP). In the OpenClaw days, every API key and external integration was tightly coupled to the agent code. Moving to MCP allowed me to abstract the data retrieval into isolated servers. I built the ESG, Market, and Policy servers as standalone entities. This meant the agents no longer needed to know *how* to fetch a stock price; they simply utilized the MCP toolset provided to them. This dramatically reduced the complexity of the agent code and made the system incredibly modular.

During this migration, security became a paramount concern. I conducted a rigorous STRIDE threat modeling exercise. I identified potential vulnerabilities: could a user use prompt injection to bypass the ESG filters and force a trade on a coal company (Spoofing/Tampering)? Could the API keys be leaked through verbose LLM error outputs (Information Disclosure)? The insights from the STRIDE model directly informed the creation of the Guard Agent. By physically separating the entity that generates the trade (Trader Agent) from the entity that enforces the rules (Guard Agent) and the entity that executes the tool (Policy MCP), I created a defense-in-depth architecture that effectively neutralized prompt injection attacks aimed at bypassing compliance.

## 5. Key Technical Decisions
Building Sentinel required several critical architectural choices that defined the system's reliability and scalability. 

**1. The Sequential ADK Graph over Dynamic Orchestration**
Early on, I experimented with dynamic agent orchestration—allowing a "manager" agent to decide which sub-agents to call on the fly. While highly flexible, it introduced unacceptable non-determinism into a financial application. A manager agent might inexplicably skip the simulation phase or, worse, bypass the climate check altogether. I made the deliberate technical decision to use ADK's `SequentialAgent` to enforce a rigid, linear pipeline. In Sentinel, every request *must* flow through Climate -> Financial -> Simulation -> Portfolio -> Trader -> Guard. This guarantees that no trade is ever executed without full environmental and financial vetting. Predictability in financial tech is a feature, not a bug.

**2. MCP Servers vs. Direct API Tooling**
The decision to implement the Model Context Protocol (MCP) rather than binding direct Python functions to the ADK agents was pivotal. Direct binding is easier initially, but it tightly couples the agent's runtime environment with the dependencies of every external API. By moving the Alpaca API calls, the ESG data fetching, and the policy enforcement into separate MCP servers communicating over STDIO, I created a true microservice architecture for the agents. If the Market data provider changes, the Financial Agent's code remains completely untouched; only the Market MCP server is updated.

**3. The "Guard-Before-Trade" Design**
Perhaps the most important security decision was the positioning of the Guard Agent. In many AI architectures, safety rails are implemented as pre-prompts or output parsers. In Sentinel, the safety layer is an independent, sovereign agent positioned immediately prior to execution. The Trader Agent is given full autonomy to propose *any* trade it believes is mathematically sound. However, the Trader Agent lacks the permissions to execute. It must submit its proposal to the Guard Agent. The Guard Agent operates on a strictly constrained prompt (`GUARD_INSTRUCTION`) and utilizes the Policy MCP server to check against hardcoded rules (e.g., maximum portfolio concentration, excluded tickers). If the Guard Agent flags a violation, it blocks the pipeline and writes to the audit log. This separation ensures that even if the Trader Agent hallucinates or is compromised by a malicious user prompt, the system remains safe.

**4. Defaulting Parameters to Prevent Conversational Loops**
A major hurdle with sequential pipelines is handling underspecified user prompts. If a user asks, "Analyze TSLA," the portfolio agent would naturally pause to ask, "What is your budget?" Because of the sequential nature, this would trigger a frustrating conversational loop where the system blocked execution to ask clarifying questions. The technical fix was embedding a "CRITICAL PIPELINE RULE" directly into the ADK instructions and setting default parameters (e.g., `budget: float = 10000.0`) in the tool signatures. This forces the LLMs to fall back on sensible defaults (like a "moderate" risk profile) rather than halting the pipeline, ensuring a seamless, single-turn user experience.

## 6. Results & Demo: End-to-End Execution
The culmination of this architecture is a system that feels surprisingly intuitive yet operates with profound complexity under the hood. When a user interacts with the Sentinel ADK Dev UI, they experience a seamless, single-turn interaction.

For example, a user submits the prompt: *"Analyze TSLA for a climate-aware investment. My ESG threshold is 78. Should I buy it?"*

Behind the scenes, the ADK pipeline roars to life. The Climate Agent immediately pings the ESG MCP Server, discovering that TSLA has a strong carbon footprint but an ESG score of 60.0—falling short of the user's strict 78 threshold. The Financial Agent simultaneously fetches the live ticker price of $215.00 via the Market MCP Server. The Simulation Agent runs its Monte Carlo projections, and the Portfolio Agent attempts an allocation. 

The Trader Agent, recognizing the user's intent to buy, formats a paper trade proposal. However, the context now hits the Guard Agent. The Guard Agent evaluates the trade against the user's threshold and the active system policies. Recognizing the ESG violation (60.0 < 78), the Guard Agent immediately blocks the execution, logging the decision as an "ESG Policy Violation" in the local audit trail.

Finally, the Explain Agent synthesizes this entire journey. The user receives a comprehensive, natural-language report detailing TSLA's exact carbon metrics, the simulated financial outlook, and a clear explanation of why the Guard Agent blocked the trade to protect their climate-aware mandate. The system successfully prevented an investment that violated the user's environmental principles, operating entirely autonomously. Sentinel successfully transforms complex, multi-domain data into secure, actionable financial decisions.

## 7. Future Work
While Sentinel 2.0 demonstrates the viability of multi-agent climate investing, the roadmap ahead is exciting. Future iterations will focus on expanding the MCP ecosystem. I plan to integrate a "News Sentiment" MCP server to ingest real-time climate policy changes and natural disaster reports, allowing the agents to proactively rebalance portfolios ahead of market shocks. Additionally, transitioning the MCP servers from local STDIO execution to remote SSE (Server-Sent Events) deployments will allow Sentinel to scale into a fully distributed cloud architecture, enabling hundreds of concurrent agent pipelines to monitor global markets in real-time.
